Three signals, three trajectories.

May closes as a month of split signals. The body composition story is genuinely strong — two scans on the same morning both showing VFA at 44–46 cm², Bio Age 31, BWI 9.0, skeletal muscle 44+ kg. The cardiovascular story is harder. Compared to January 2026 — the most recent stable reference point before the programme — RHR is 3.9 bpm higher (66.5 vs 62.6), HRR is 4.5 bpm/min lower (32.4 vs 36.9), and VO2 is 4.5 points lower (42.8 vs 47.3). The aerobic remodelling just hasn't arrived yet.

The gap between January and now tells the programme story clearly. January was a good-but-not-great month: RHR in the low 60s, HRR near 37, VO2 at 47 — all meaningfully better than today. The task is not to reach the Apr 2025 peak immediately, but to close the gap to January first. The ceiling is intact.

April 13 produced a 70 ms HRV reading — the highest since August 2025. The prior day was a heavy training day (20,219 steps, 204 exercise minutes). The night that followed was a low wrist-temperature night of 34.95°C. This is what the autonomic nervous system can produce when conditions align. The system is not broken — it is being given too few opportunities to express itself.

The cardiovascular triangle.

Three metrics define cardiovascular fitness and they move together. RHR, HRR, and VO2 are mathematically linked — when you train aerobically with sufficient stimulus, the heart becomes more efficient: it pumps more blood per beat (lowering RHR), recovers faster after exertion (raising HRR), and delivers oxygen more effectively (raising VO2). When the stimulus disappears, all three drift in the wrong direction simultaneously. The data confirms this with clarity: RHR up 18%, HRR down 31%, VO2 down 12% across the 15-month window.

Compared to January 2026 — the reference month — the picture is mixed. HRR at 32.4 bpm/min is below January's 36.9 (−4.5) but has held above the 30 bpm/min clinical threshold for the full month of May. VO2 at 42.8 vs January's 47.3 (−4.5) is the metric that most needs attention. The relationship between HRR and VO2 in this data (r = 0.73) is one of the strongest in the dataset — they will recover together. The prescription: sustained aerobic stimulus at moderate-to-vigorous intensity, three to four sessions weekly, for at least six weeks before expecting movement in either number. Week 5 of 13 is exactly where that stimulus should be compounding.

HRV is the exception to the January gap. At 42.0 ms vs January's 36.2, it is 5.8 ms better — the highest monthly average since June 2025. The autonomic system is clearly responding to the improved recovery discipline and sleep quality. The RHR–HRV correlation across the dataset is r = −0.41 — meaningful, but weaker than the RHR–VO2 link. HRV responding while VO2 lags is consistent with the theory: HRV reflects nervous system recovery (faster), VO2 reflects cardiac remodelling (slower, 8–12 week lag).

Social hours context · confounding explained

Social hours correlate positively with good health metrics — not because socialising improves HRV, but because high-social months coincide with periods when life is balanced overall: lower office load, yoga present, training consistent.

The exception that reveals the mechanism: Sep 2025 had high social hours (35h) driven by evening drinks sessions — and the worst health metrics in the dataset. Total hours obscure what's actually happening. Quality and context of social time matters, not raw volume.

What the Life Cycle data reveals that Apple Health cannot.

The Apple Health data shows what happened to the body. The Life Cycle data shows what happened in the life that caused it. Three patterns stand out when the two datasets are mapped together.

Yoga is the central variable, not a peripheral one. The r = +0.78 correlation between monthly yoga hours and VO2 Max is the strongest relationship in the combined dataset — stronger than any workout metric, any sleep metric, any other lifestyle factor. The dose-response table makes this concrete: zero yoga months average RHR 63.6 bpm and VO2 46.9. Months with 15–40 hours of yoga average RHR 55.3 and VO2 51.0. That is a 9-point VO2 difference and a full 8 bpm of RHR purely from one lifestyle variable. April 2025 — 61 hours of yoga, your best health readings in the dataset — is not a coincidence.

The office hours picture is more nuanced than it appears. High office hours correlate weakly with worse RHR (r = −0.38) but the relationship is not linear. The months with over 130 office hours (Aug, Nov 2025) don't have the worst metrics — September does, at 132 hours, but the damage came from the combination of high office hours plus zero yoga plus heavy social load. Office hours alone are not the problem. Office hours in the absence of recovery infrastructure is the problem. The months where office hours are moderate (90–110) and yoga is present have the best health outcomes of the dataset.

The corrected drinks data removes 14 false positives — the Manly Wharf ferry terminal entries — and reveals the drinks trend more clearly. September 2025's 22 hours of actual drinking (down from the previously-calculated 22.4) was genuinely exceptional. The 2026 pattern (3–6 hours per month) represents a structural shift rather than temporary noise. The session-duration data is the most actionable finding: sessions under 30 minutes produce next-day HRV of 39.8 ms — essentially baseline. Sessions over 2 hours produce 34.7 ms — an 18% suppression. Frequency matters far less than duration per occasion.

What actually drives the core metrics

A note on what these correlations show.

This page presents two views of the same data. The correlation bars show how metrics co-move across the entire 15-month dataset. The driver hierarchy ranks impact based on patterns visible in monthly aggregates and within-period comparisons.

One critical caveat: most of the strong-looking raw correlations between sleep stages and cardiovascular metrics are time confounds, not behavioural levers. As the injury and decline period unfolded, deep sleep increased while RHR rose — they co-moved, but neither caused the other. Within a given month, day-to-day sleep duration has near-zero relationship to next-day RHR or HRV (within-month r ≈ 0.0). The behavioural drivers identified in this analysis — training intensity portfolio, recovery practices, sustained yoga absence — operate on weekly-to-monthly timescales. They are visible in the period benchmarks, not in daily correlations.

What the relationships actually mean.

The cleanest finding is the cardiovascular triangle: RHR, HRR, and VO2 are tightly bound to each other. RHR–VO2 at −0.73 and HRR–VO2 at +0.73 are very strong correlations by behavioural data standards. They mean these three metrics will move together. The strategy is one: sustained moderate-vigorous aerobic stimulus, multiple sessions per week, for at least six weeks.

HRV sits slightly apart. It's only modestly correlated with RHR (−0.41) and weakly with VO2 (+0.26). HRV is measuring something different — the autonomic nervous system's regulatory capacity at any given moment, which depends on factors beyond cardiac fitness alone. HRV responds to recovery practices that don't show up in the cardiovascular triangle: recovery discipline, sleep consistency, and reduced lifestyle stress. This is why the prescription for HRV improvement is genuinely different from the prescription for VO2 improvement.

Walking asymmetry's modest but real correlations (−0.18 with RHR, +0.21 with VO2) suggest the knee constraint has had compounding effects. The benefit of its recovery won't show up immediately — it shows up by removing a constraint, allowing future training to build adaptation rather than fight compensation patterns.

Body composition is a genuine bright spot.

Six scans in, the body composition picture is consistent and encouraging. VFA has traced a clear downward trend: 50→37→34→55→49→45 cm² — the S4 spike was a hydration artefact, and the underlying direction is unambiguous. BWI back to 9.0 in Scan 6, Bio Age holding at 31, skeletal muscle stable at 44+ kg across all morning scans. Compared to January 2026, body composition is the one area where there is no gap to close — the scan series confirms the structural foundation is intact. The cardiovascular deterioration that began in Q3 2025 is not a body composition story. It is a training stimulus story.

The most reliable metric in the series is visceral fat area, which has declined consistently: 50 → 37 → 34 cm² across three scans taken under different hydration conditions. This directional consistency across varying protocols is the strongest evidence available from BIA technology. Visceral fat responds quickly to reductions in systemic inflammation and improved metabolic load — both of which are consistent with the lifestyle changes made since March 2026.

The waist-to-hip ratio has moved consistently: 0.78 → 0.72 → 0.71 → 0.80 → 0.77 → 0.76. The S4 spike (0.80) was hydration-driven; S5 and S6 confirm the recovery. This is a genuine reduction in central fat distribution independent of hydration variance. The body composition foundation is strong. The gap between it and the cardiovascular metrics is the defining story of this dataset.

Phase 01 · Foundation
Weeks 1–4
  • Add 10 min daily slow breathwork (5-in / 5-out)
  • Prioritise 5–6 structured recovery days per week
  • Circuit training / skipping 3× per week, 15–20 min sessions
  • Surfing 2× per week (already established)
  • Add 1 cycling session weekly — easy effort, 30 min
  • Caffeine cutoff by 1pm daily
Phase 02 · Build
Weeks 5–8
  • Circuit training volume to 30 min total per week
  • Self-powered cycling 2× per week if available, build to 45 min sessions
  • Structured rest day with deliberate recovery (no training)
  • Replace 2 walking sessions/week with Zone 3 effort
  • Scan 3 between weeks 5–6, properly standardised
  • Mid-point review: RHR and HRR trajectories
Phase 03 · Consolidate
Weeks 9–12
  • Zone 3+ volume to 90+ min weekly, sustained
  • Extend Zone 3 sessions to 30+ min as fitness allows
  • Maintain recovery discipline · re-evaluate at week 12
  • End-of-period scan · compare to scan 3
  • Full data review · set targets for Q3 2026
  • If HRR still <33 — book GP review

What success looks like at week 12.

If the plan is followed with reasonable adherence, the dataset by late July should show: RHR monthly average around 60 bpm, with multiple sub-58 days per week. HRV monthly average above 44 ms, with the 70 ms ceiling visible on multiple days per week rather than monthly. HRR climbing back toward the mid-30s, comfortably above the 30 bpm/min threshold. VO2 stabilised above 45 and ideally trending toward 47–48.

The mobility metrics — which are already strong — should hold. Walking asymmetry will likely settle in the 0.5–0.7% range. Body composition, with proper scan conditions, will reveal where muscle and fat composition truly sits. Sleep architecture, currently at its best, has a strong chance of staying there given the current lifestyle direction and improved recovery practices.

The most important deliverable from these 90 days is not any single number. It is establishing whether the cardiovascular triangle responds. Either RHR, HRR, and VO2 move together with the prescribed intervention — in which case the path forward is more of the same — or they don't, in which case there is a deeper signal worth investigating with a clinician. The 90-day window is sufficient to know which scenario applies.
